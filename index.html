WHAT IS AN

OLD POPULATION?

The OECD identifies the "Elderly population [as] the share of the population aged 65 and over".(Ref) Countries are considered to have old populations if their elderly population is increasing at a faster rate than their total population.

Let us look to the island nation of Japan for a glimpse of this demographic shift. Japan's Population over 65 as of 2022 OECD Elderly Population statistics is 29% of its population, compared to the OECD average of just 17.96%, and to OECD countries with younger populations having a population over 65 as low as 8% in the case of Mexico!

So what causes an old population?
Countries with old or ageing populations are expereincing a bitter-sweet combination of increasing life expectancies and decreasing fertility rates. In our case study on Japan we can see that the country's Fertility Rate, measured by the number of children born per woman in a year, is 1.3 as of 2021 (ref). This number is problematic as the OECD recongnises 2.1 children per woman as necessary to maintain a stable population, when accounting for no net migration and unchanged morality (reference)

While the 2021 OECD average for Life Expectancy at birth was 80.3 years (ref), Japan recorded life expectancy at birth as 84.5 years, more than 4 years longer than the OECD average! This combination of having fewer children and living longer has left Japan with one of the oldest populations that is set to get even older
